



There is evidence to suggest that language plays a pivotal role in the development of cognitive abilities, including consciousness, self-awareness, and understanding of time. Investigating feral children, or those who have experienced isolation from human contact during their early years and have had little to no exposure to language, can offer valuable insights into the influence of language on cognitive development.

One well-known case of feral children is Genie, a girl discovered in 1970 at the age of 13 after being confined in a small room and isolated from human interaction for most of her life. Upon her discovery, Genie could not speak, and her cognitive abilities were severely impaired. Researchers spent years working with Genie to teach her language and rehabilitate her cognitive abilities. Despite progress, she never fully acquired a normal level of language or cognitive functioning. Genie's case indicates that language development is crucial for the development of consciousness and other cognitive abilities.

Victor, the "Wild Boy of Aveyron," is another famous case of feral children. Found in France in the late 18th century, Victor had limited exposure to language and social interaction. Despite efforts by physician Jean-Marc Gaspard Itard, Victor never fully acquired language. His case also supports the idea that language development is essential for cognitive development and self-awareness.

Susan Goldin-Meadow and her colleagues conducted a study in 2005 with deaf children in Nicaragua that further substantiates the importance of language in cognitive development. These children, lacking access to formal sign language, developed their own "homesign" system to communicate. The researchers found that the children's understanding of time and other abstract concepts was limited, but it improved as their homesign system became more sophisticated.

In conclusion, although there is no direct evidence that feral children develop a sense of consciousness through language development, studies and case histories do suggest that language plays a critical role in developing cognitive abilities. The cases of Genie, Victor, and the deaf children in Nicaragua demonstrate that language deprivation significantly impacts cognitive development. Further research is necessary to fully comprehend the relationship between language and cognitive development, but it seems apparent that language is a vital component in the development of consciousness, self-awareness, and understanding of time.
